Fractals,

What are they?

A Brief Description

Fractals are mathematical shapes that are infinite in complexity and surface area, but finite in volume or area. One could argue that the most fascenating part of these shapes is the fact that while they are infinitly complex, their structuring is based on simple recursive equations

These marvels were originally theorised by a plethora of researchers and mathematicians in the early 19th century. The first of these was known as the cantor set, which was discovered by Georg Cantor in 1886. It is comprised of a line segment that is trisected and the middle is removed. Then, for the two remaining line segments the process is continued, resulting in a shape known as a cantor set.



Many other fractals have also been discovered over the years. One of the most famous of which being the Mandelbrot set, which was discovered by Benoit B. Mandelbrot, a French mathematician. This discovery was the pandora's box in terms of fractal discovery. After this point, computing power increased thus allowing people to discover a larger variaty of these shapes and volumes.



Some of the most awe-inspiring fractals currently occupy the third dimension. These fractals are commonly referred to as Mandelbulbs. Similar to their two-dimensional counterparts, these fractals are infinitly complex. However, because these fractals occupy three dimensions, they require a spherical coordinate system rather than the traditional cartesian one. This simply means that the output is plotted relative to the distance around a point in three dimensional space.
